# Chapter 10: Quiet

The late morning light in Blackspire was thin and colorless.

It entered the high windows in narrow bands and faded before it reached the center of the corridors, leaving the stone in its usual quiet dusk. Lucian walked alone through one of the upper halls, his steps soft against the worn floor. The banners hung still along the walls, their silver swords and black wings catching what little brightness there was before the light failed. The cold sat in the walls the way it always did — not sharp, not sudden, simply present, as if the keep had never fully accepted the idea of warmth. Nothing in the place announced itself. It simply continued.

He passed a pair of household staff moving in the opposite direction with a covered tray. They stepped aside at once, eyes lowered, and waited in silence until he had gone by before resuming their work. Further on, the distant ring of metal reached him from somewhere deeper in the mountain — ordered, repetitive, the sound of training that never fully ceased. Lucian did not turn toward it. He kept to the quieter path, the one that followed the outer edge of the residential wing where the windows looked down across the lower terraces and the long white drop of the peak.

The house felt large around him. It always did. The pillars rose far above his head, carved with the same severe elegance that marked every formal space in Blackspire. The silence between the occasional footsteps had weight. He had lived inside this silence long enough that it no longer surprised him, yet it never became entirely ordinary. The stone remembered. Or at least it felt that way to him — as if every person who had ever walked these corridors had left some faint pressure behind, and the keep held it without comment.

Memories from recent days moved under the surface of the morning without urgency. The press of heat in the forges, dense and foreign after the upper cold. The pale gleam of Cold Silver running through dark rock. The living temperature of black scales under his hand, smooth and cool at the surface, warmer beneath. The sudden expansion of the world when Ancalion’s wings caught the wind and the keep fell away beneath them. The vast silver presence of Lirath at the mouth of the ancient cave, settled and absolute. The faint, unnamed depth farther back in the mountain, a pressure in the silence that did not belong to either dragon. None of it demanded attention. It simply stayed with him, settled into the same quiet place as everything else the house had already given him to carry.

He turned down a narrower passage that led toward one of the secondary training platforms. The air grew colder as the walls closed in. Frost clung to the outer edges of the stone where the wind found its way through the narrow openings. The light here was even thinner. Lucian adjusted his cloak without thinking and continued, his footsteps the only sound in the passage until the platform opened ahead of him.

Dain was already there.

He stood near the open edge of the platform, looking out across the pale slopes. His posture was the same as always — upright, exact, unhurried. He did not turn at once when Lucian stepped into view. He finished whatever private measure he was taking of the land, then looked down at the boy with the same controlled expression he wore in the training halls.

“Come here.”

Lucian crossed the stone and stopped at the proper distance. The wind moved freely across the platform, sharper than in the sheltered corridors, carrying the clean mineral scent of high rock and snow.

Dain studied him for a moment in silence. His gaze moved once from Lucian’s face to his stance and back again, as if confirming that the boy who stood before him was still the one he had been training.

“You were seen on Ancalion.”

The words were plain. There was no heat in them, no raised voice, no unnecessary weight. Only the same practical finality Dain used when correcting a stance or ending a form that had been performed incorrectly.

Lucian did not look away. “Yes, Uncle.”

“You are still too young.” Dain’s voice remained even. “The bond does not change that. Certain things are not yet permitted. When you are seen doing what has not been authorized, questions follow. The house prefers not to answer questions it has not chosen to raise.”

Lucian waited. The wind pulled at the edge of his cloak.

“The dragon is yours,” Dain continued. “That is already known. How far you ride him, and when, is not yet yours to decide. Discipline still applies. Especially to the heir.”

A short silence stretched between them. Below the platform the slopes fell away in long pale bands. Somewhere far down the mountain a hawk turned against the steel-colored sky and was gone.

“I understand,” Lucian said.

Dain gave a single nod, as though the matter had been recorded and closed.

“Return to the upper forms. Continue as you have been. We will increase the work when your endurance improves. Until then, the sky can wait.”

He turned his attention back toward the slopes, the conversation finished as cleanly as it had begun. Lucian inclined his head and left the platform without further word.

The corridors felt unchanged when he re-entered them.

The same thin light. The same cold in the stone. The same quiet movement of the household at its careful distance. The reprimand had been light and exact, and it left no sharp edge behind — only the clear knowledge that a boundary had been marked and that he was expected to hold it. Lucian did not resist the knowledge. He did not turn it over for hidden meanings or softer interpretations. He simply carried it the way he carried the rest.

He walked without destination for a time, letting the keep move around him. A steward passed with a ledger under one arm and gave the proper acknowledgment before continuing on. Somewhere below, a door closed with a heavy, controlled sound that echoed briefly and then was swallowed by the stone. From an open arch he caught a brief glimpse of the lower terraces where a small detachment of Silver Guard moved in formation, their Cold Silver plate catching what little light the sky offered before they passed out of view. Their steps were exact. Their silence matched the keep’s.

The ordinary life of Blackspire continued.

Lucian stopped at a high window that looked north. The glass was cold under his fingertips. Beyond it the peaks stood in their long silence, white and gray beneath the steel-colored sky. The drop from the window was severe. He could see the upper reaches of the Ascend far below, a pale line cut into the mountain, and beyond it the distant suggestion of Firsthold in the valley. The memory of the low glide returned for a moment — the rush of air against his face, the living strength of Ancalion beneath him, the sudden smallness of the keep when seen from the open wing — and then settled again. Beside it sat Dain’s words, sparse and final. Beside both sat everything else the house had already placed in his keeping: the weight of the name, the cold of the stone, the quiet expectation that he would one day be equal to all of it.

He did not name any of it. He only stood there until the cold of the glass began to reach his skin through the glove, then stepped back into the corridor and continued on.

The keep did not mark his passing. It simply continued as it always had: ordered, severe, and quiet. Lucian moved through it the same way, carrying what he had been given, adding the newest weight to the others without ceremony or complaint.

The day went on around him, thin light and cold stone, as if nothing of note had occurred at all.
