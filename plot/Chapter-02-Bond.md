# Chapter 2: Bond

The wind had not changed.

Lucian still stood beside his father at the edge of the terrace, the vast cold spread of the Northern Duchy open before them. The weight of Vesper’s words remained in the air between them, unsettled and heavy. For a moment longer, neither spoke. The mountains held their silence. Far below, the pale rivers of ice lay motionless under the steel-colored sky, and the distant forests looked more like shadows than living things. Even the banners of the keep, somewhere behind them, seemed part of another world.

Lucian’s fingers had gone stiff inside his cloak. He flexed them once, slowly, and let them still again. He did not mention the cold.

Then a sound rose from beyond the peaks.

It began low — so low it was almost felt before it was heard — a deep, resonant call that moved through the stone beneath their feet and into the chest. There was a cold edge to it, something sharp beneath the depth, as though the cry itself carried winter. It did not scatter in the open air. It pressed inward, steady and certain, until it seemed to belong as much to the mountain as to the sky.

Lucian’s breath caught.

He did not know the word for what moved through him, only that he recognized it completely. The same quiet pressure he had felt when looking out over the land now answered from above the clouds. It was not a sound that belonged to the keep, or the gardens, or the men who walked the walls. It belonged to him. The certainty of that settled in his ribs before he had time to question it, as natural as the next breath.

“Ancalion,” he said.

Vesper’s gaze lifted.

The clouds above the highest ridges tore open. A dark shape descended through them, wings stretched wide against the pale sky. Even young, the dragon was striking — black scales without sheen, a body still lean with youth, but the wings already large in proportion, cutting the air with clean, powerful strokes. As he came lower, the deep purple of his eyes became visible, vivid against the dark of his form, fixed and intent.

Ancalion swept once over the terrace. The force of his passage stirred Lucian’s cloak and sent a thin spray of frost lifting from the stone. The air itself seemed to shift around the movement. Then the dragon curved and came down beyond the low wall, claws finding purchase with controlled weight. The impact sent a faint tremor through the terrace. He folded his wings and went still, the last traces of the cold cry fading into the mountain air. Steam rose faintly from his breath into the cold.

For a moment, no one moved.

Vesper studied the dragon in silence. His expression did not soften, but something focused entered his eyes — the same measuring look he sometimes turned on Lucian.

“He grows quickly,” Vesper said at last. His voice was calm, almost distant. “Faster than expected. There is strength in him already. Potential.”

He looked down at his son.

“See that you do not waste it.”

Lucian said nothing. He only nodded once. The words settled beside the earlier ones — burden, crown, weight — and did not contradict them.

Vesper held his gaze a moment longer, then turned away from the terrace edge.

“I have matters to attend to,” he said. “Do not remain long. The wind is rising.”

His footsteps faded back toward the Black Halls, measured and unhurried, until the quiet of the mountain returned and the space he had occupied felt suddenly larger for his absence. Even the air seemed to loosen slightly without him in it.

Only then did Lucian step forward.

Ancalion lowered his head as he approached. The dragon’s breath steamed in the cold, dark and warm at once. Lucian lifted a hand and set it against the side of the long neck, fingers spreading over scales that felt like cooled metal. The moment he touched him, the last of the distance between them vanished.

There was no need for words. The bond was already there — older than understanding, deeper than speech. Lucian felt the steady presence of the dragon the way he felt his own pulse. Not as something added, but as something that had always been waiting to be noticed. It did not ask for anything. It simply answered. Where his father’s words had pressed down, this rose to meet them.

Ancalion shifted closer, the great head angling until one deep purple eye regarded him at near level. In it, Lucian saw no reflection of fear or hesitation. Only recognition. The kind that did not need to be earned in the moment because it had already been decided long before.

He stood like that for a long time, hand against black scales, the cold wind moving around them both. Snow-dust drifted across the terrace stones in thin, restless lines. The land below no longer seemed quite so endless. The weight his father had spoken of remained — the peaks, the valleys, the people, the fear, the responsibility — but it no longer stood alone. There was something beside it now. Something living. Something that answered when he reached for it without thinking.

Lucian let his forehead rest briefly against the dragon’s neck. The scales were cold at first, then not. He closed his eyes for a moment and simply breathed. In the quiet, the bond felt less like a connection and more like a fact.

Soft footsteps approached from behind.

“There you are.”

Elara’s voice was quiet, but it carried easily in the still air. Lucian turned. His mother stood a short distance away, silver hair stirred by the wind, her cloak drawn close against the cold. Her expression was gentle as her eyes moved from him to the dragon and back again.

She came closer without hurry. When she reached them, she stopped beside her son and looked up at Ancalion for a moment. The dragon did not pull away. He only watched her, calm and still, as if her presence required no decision from him.

“He came for you,” she said.

Lucian’s hand remained against the black scales. “I felt him.”

Elara nodded, as if that single answer held everything she needed. She reached out and brushed a strand of dark hair back from his forehead, her fingers warm against his skin. The touch was light, familiar, and steadying in a way that asked for nothing in return.

“You looked very much like your father just now,” she said softly. “Standing there with him.”

Lucian did not know how to answer. He only leaned, almost without thinking, into the touch of her hand. The cold of the terrace, the height of the keep, and the long fall of the mountains seemed to draw back a little. For a moment there was only the three of them: the boy, the dragon, and the mother who had come to find them.

Elara let her hand rest lightly against his hair a while longer before she spoke again.

“Come,” she said. “It is growing colder.”

Lucian hesitated, then nodded. He stepped back from Ancalion, though the bond did not thin with the distance. It remained, quiet and sure, like a second pulse beneath his own. The dragon stayed by the wall, watching him, wings still half-settled against his sides.

Elara held out her hand. When Lucian took it, the warmth felt different than it had in the corridors that morning — quieter, steadier.

They turned toward the Black Halls.

Behind them, Ancalion opened his wings. The broad black spans caught the cold air, and with a single powerful motion he pushed away from the stone. The same deep, resonant cry rose from him as he climbed — low at first, then sharper, carrying that cold edge. It echoed once against the peaks before the clouds began to take him back.

Lucian did not look over his shoulder.

He did not need to.

The bond held.

They walked in silence for a time, the stone path leading them back toward the arched entrance of the keep. The wind lessened as the walls rose around them again, its sharp edge breaking against dark stone. Frost still clung to the outer steps in thin white seams, cracking faintly under Lucian’s boots. Inside, the temperature shifted only slightly, but the air no longer cut. It smelled of cold rock, old iron, and the faint smoke that always seemed to live in the deeper halls of Blackspire.

Lucian’s hand remained in his mother’s. Her fingers were warm where his had gone stiff from the terrace wind. For a few steps he simply let that warmth exist, without trying to name what it changed.

After a while, Elara spoke.

“You were not afraid of him.”

Lucian glanced up. The dim light from the high windows caught in her silver hair as they walked. “Of Ancalion?”

“Yes.”

He shook his head once. “No.”

Elara’s mouth curved slightly. “Most children would have been.”

“I knew him,” Lucian said. Then, after a moment, more carefully: “I could feel him.”

She did not question that. She only adjusted her hold on his hand, drawing it a little further into the shelter of her cloak so the remaining cold could not reach his fingers so easily. The fabric of her sleeve brushed his wrist, soft against the harsher textures of the keep.

Their footsteps sounded softer now that they were fully inside. The corridor stretched ahead in a long, measured line. Dark banners hung motionless along the walls, the silver swords and black wings repeating in silence above them. Somewhere farther down the hall, a door closed. The sound carried farther than it should have in the stillness, then faded.

“Your father watched him closely,” she said.

“I know.”

“He watches you the same way.”

Lucian was quiet for a few steps. The stone beneath his boots held the cold stubbornly, seeping up through the soles even here. He could still feel the memory of the wind on his face, and beneath it, quieter than either, the steady presence of the bond he had left behind on the terrace. It had not lessened with distance. If anything, the walls made it clearer by contrast.

“Does that mean he is pleased?” he asked.

Elara’s answer did not come at once. A faint draft moved through the corridor, stirring the lower edge of a banner as they passed. The silver thread of the sigil caught the light for an instant, then dulled again.

“It means he is paying attention,” she said. “With your father, that is not a small thing.”

Lucian considered this. It did not feel like comfort, exactly. But it did not feel empty either. Attention, from Vesper, had always carried weight. Today that weight felt less like judgment and more like measure.

“I will not waste it,” he said.

Elara looked down at him, and for a moment her expression softened further. The pale light touched the side of her face, making her look less like a figure of the keep and more like the warmth that had led him through it that morning.

“I know,” she said. “That is what troubles me, sometimes.”

He did not understand that fully. The words stayed with him anyway. Before he could ask, she gave his hand a gentle squeeze and turned her gaze ahead again.

They passed deeper into the shelter of the hall. The last traces of terrace cold still clung to his cloak, but her presence made it easier to ignore. The keep felt larger around them now — high, dark, and listening — yet less severe than it had before his father left them on the mountain’s edge. Or perhaps he had simply changed enough, in the space of a single morning, to stand differently inside it.

“There is someone already waiting for you,” she said.

Lucian looked up. “Who?”

Her smile was small, and warm.

“Your sister.”
