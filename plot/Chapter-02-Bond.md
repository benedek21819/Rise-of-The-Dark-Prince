# Chapter 2: Bond

The wind had not changed.

Lucian still stood beside his father at the edge of the terrace, the vast cold spread of the Northern Duchy open before them. The weight of Vesper’s words remained in the air between them, unsettled and heavy. For a moment longer, neither spoke.

Then a sound rose from beyond the peaks.

It began low — so low it was almost felt before it was heard — a deep, resonant call that moved through the stone beneath their feet and into the chest. There was a cold edge to it, something sharp beneath the depth, as though the cry itself carried winter.

Lucian’s breath caught.

He did not know the word for what moved through him, only that he recognized it completely. The same quiet pressure he had felt when looking out over the land now answered from above the clouds.

“Ancalion,” he said.

Vesper’s gaze lifted.

The clouds above the highest ridges tore open. A dark shape descended through them, wings stretched wide against the pale sky. Even young, the dragon was striking — black scales without sheen, a body still lean with youth, but the wings already large in proportion, cutting the air with clean, powerful strokes. As he came lower, the deep purple of his eyes became visible, vivid against the dark of his form.

Ancalion swept once over the terrace, the force of his passage stirring Lucian’s cloak, then curved and came down beyond the low wall, claws finding stone with controlled weight. The impact sent a faint tremor through the terrace. The dragon folded his wings and went still, the last traces of the cold cry fading into the mountain air.

For a moment, no one moved.

Vesper studied the dragon in silence. His expression did not soften, but something focused entered his eyes — the same measuring look he sometimes turned on Lucian.

“He grows quickly,” Vesper said at last. His voice was calm, almost distant. “Faster than expected. There is strength in him already. Potential.”

He looked down at his son.

“See that you do not waste it.”

Lucian said nothing. He only nodded once.

Vesper held his gaze a moment longer, then turned away from the terrace edge.

“I have matters to attend to,” he said. “Do not remain long. The wind is rising.”

His footsteps faded back toward the Black Halls, measured and unhurried, until the quiet of the mountain returned.

Only then did Lucian step forward.

Ancalion lowered his head as he approached. The dragon’s breath steamed in the cold, dark and warm at once. Lucian lifted a hand and set it against the side of the long neck, fingers spreading over scales that felt like cooled metal. The moment he touched him, the last of the distance between them vanished.

There was no need for words. The bond was already there — older than understanding, deeper than speech. Lucian felt the steady presence of the dragon the way he felt his own pulse. Ancalion shifted closer, the great head angling until one deep purple eye regarded him at near level. In it, Lucian saw no reflection of fear or hesitation. Only recognition.

He stood like that for a long time, hand against black scales, the cold wind moving around them both. The land below no longer seemed quite so endless. The weight his father had spoken of remained, but it no longer stood alone.

Soft footsteps approached from behind.

“There you are.”

Elara’s voice was quiet, but it carried easily in the still air. Lucian turned. His mother stood a short distance away, silver hair stirred by the wind, her expression gentle as her eyes moved from him to the dragon and back again.

She came closer without hurry. When she reached them, she stopped beside her son and looked up at Ancalion for a moment. The dragon did not pull away. He only watched her, calm and still.

“He came for you,” she said.

Lucian’s hand remained against the black scales. “I felt him.”

Elara nodded, as if that single answer held everything she needed. She reached out and brushed a strand of dark hair back from his forehead, her fingers warm against his skin.

“You looked very much like your father just now,” she said softly. “Standing there with him.”

Lucian did not know how to answer. He only leaned, almost without thinking, into the touch of her hand. The cold of the terrace, the height of the keep, and the long fall of the mountains seemed to draw back a little. For a moment there was only the three of them: the boy, the dragon, and the mother who had come to find them.

Elara let her hand rest lightly against his hair a while longer before she spoke again.

“Come,” she said. “It is growing colder.”

Lucian hesitated, then nodded. He stepped back from Ancalion, though the bond did not thin with the distance. The dragon remained by the wall, watching him.

Elara held out her hand. When Lucian took it, the warmth felt different than it had in the corridors that morning — quieter, steadier.

They turned toward the Black Halls.

Behind them, Ancalion opened his wings. The broad black spans caught the cold air, and with a single powerful motion he pushed away from the stone. The same deep, resonant cry rose from him as he climbed — low at first, then sharper, carrying that cold edge. It echoed once against the peaks before the clouds began to take him back.

Lucian did not look over his shoulder.

He did not need to.

The bond held.
