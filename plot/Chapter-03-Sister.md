# Chapter 3: Sister

The corridors seemed narrower after the open air of the terrace.

Lucian walked beside his mother in silence, his hand still in hers. The cold of the mountain had not fully left his cloak or the tips of his fingers. Frost still clung faintly to the outer folds of the fabric, melting in slow dark patches as the deeper air of the keep closed around them. Where the wetness spread, the cloth grew heavier against his shoulders. The halls of Blackspire held a different kind of stillness here — heavier, closer, shaped by stone rather than wind. Every footstep sounded smaller than it should have, as if the walls took the noise and kept it. The banners hung motionless above them, their dark cloth absorbing what little light the high windows allowed. Silver swords. Black wings. The same marks repeated until they became part of the quiet itself.

He could still feel Ancalion.

Not as strongly as when his palm had rested against black scales, but enough. A steady presence at the edge of thought, like a second pulse that did not belong entirely to his own body. Sometimes it faded almost to nothing. Sometimes, when he breathed too carefully, it returned with quiet certainty. The bond had not broken when the dragon rose back into the clouds. It had only changed distance.

Elara did not hurry him. Her steps remained even, the soft sound of her boots lost quickly in the size of the keep. When they turned from the wider passage into a quieter corridor near the family rooms, the air felt less severe. The light from the high windows fell in narrower bands here, catching on dark stone and the occasional edge of silver thread. Dust turned slowly in those thin shafts of brightness. Somewhere farther down the hall, water dripped faintly from an unseen seam in the rock. The sound was small, but in the silence it carried, regular as a soft count.

Someone was already waiting.

Lyra stood near one of the recessed arches, her hands folded loosely in front of her. She was older than Lucian by enough years that she no longer looked like a child in the same way he did — taller, more composed, her dark hair arranged neatly against the black of her sleeves, her posture already careful. The pale light touched the side of her face and made her eyes seem sharper than they were. There was no restlessness in the way she waited. Only attention. When she saw them, something in her expression eased, though she did not rush forward.

Elara slowed.

“She has been waiting,” she said quietly, more to Lucian than to the girl ahead of them. “I told her I would bring you once your father was finished.”

Lucian looked up at his mother, then toward Lyra.

Lyra stepped closer as they approached. Her gaze moved first over Lucian — his cloak, his face, the faint signs of cold still on him — before she inclined her head slightly toward Elara.

“Lady Elara,” she said.

Elara answered with a small smile and released Lucian’s hand. The absence of her fingers left the air cooler against his skin, a sudden small emptiness he noticed more than he expected to.

“I will leave you,” she said. Her hand brushed once more against his shoulder, light as before. “Do not keep him in the corridors too long.”

Then she was gone, her silver hair catching the light once before the turn of the hall took her. The soft rhythm of her steps faded, swallowed by distance and stone, and the quieter stretch of corridor belonged only to the two of them.

For a moment Lucian and Lyra stood alone.

Lyra looked down at him properly now. Up close, the concern in her eyes was clearer, though she kept it measured. She reached out without asking and adjusted the edge of his cloak where the wind had pulled it slightly askew. Her fingers were careful, practiced, as if she had done this many times before. She brushed a faint trace of moisture from the fabric near his shoulder, then smoothed it flat against him with the side of her hand.

“You’re cold,” she said.

“I’m fine.”

“That isn’t what I asked.”

There was no sharpness in the correction. Only the quiet firmness of someone already used to looking after him. She studied his face a moment longer, as if checking for something beyond the cold, then let her hand fall.

“Lady Elara said Father sent for you,” she said. “Was it important?”

Lucian thought of the terrace. Of the land spread out below them in white and steel-gray. Of the wind at the edge of the wall and the words that had settled into the air like something that would not leave.

“Yes,” he said.

Lyra did not press him for details. She only nodded once, as if that answer was enough for now, and turned slightly toward the deeper corridor.

“Come,” she said. “There’s no need to stand here.”

They walked together.

Lyra matched her pace to his without making it obvious. The difference in their height made her steps longer by nature, but she shortened them enough that he did not have to hurry. The stone underfoot held the cold stubbornly. Even through his boots Lucian could feel it, a slow seep of chill that the inner halls never fully surrendered. In the softer light of these passages, the keep felt less like a place of judgment and more like a place that simply endured — old, severe, and watching. The walls did not need to speak to make themselves known.

After a few moments, she glanced down at him again.

“You saw Ancalion.”

It was not quite a question.

“Yes.”

“He came down to the terrace.”

Lucian looked up at her. “How did you know?”

“I felt the air change,” she said simply. “And you look different when you’ve been with him.”

He did not know what to say to that. His fingers curled slightly at his side, remembering the cool weight of scales beneath them, the way the bond had answered without being asked. Even now, at this quieter distance, he could still find it if he reached.

“Father said he is growing quickly,” Lucian said after a moment.

“He is.” Lyra’s voice remained calm. “Everyone says so. Even the guards talk about it when they think no one is listening. They say his wings are already larger than they should be for his age.”

“Father said there is potential in him.”

“There is potential in both of you.” She said it without ceremony, as if it were an ordinary fact. “That is why he watches so closely.”

Lucian was quiet for a few steps. The corridor narrowed slightly, then opened again. A tall window to their left let in a band of pale light that crossed the floor in a clean white line. When they passed through it, the brightness touched his face and was gone.

“Does he watch you the same way?” he asked.

Lyra’s mouth moved in something too small to be called a smile.

“Not the same way,” she said. “You are the heir. That is a different kind of attention.”

They turned into a narrower passage that led toward the family wing. The stone here was darker, the banners fewer. The air smelled faintly of oil and cold iron, the permanent scent of a keep that never truly slept. Somewhere ahead, the low sound of a door closing echoed once and then faded into the walls, leaving the silence thicker than before.

“Was Father severe?” Lyra asked.

Lucian considered the word. The memory of Vesper’s voice on the terrace returned without effort — even, quiet, and heavy enough to remain after the sound itself was gone. He remembered the way the land had looked from the edge of the wall, too large for his hands, and the way the motto had settled into the cold as if it had always belonged there.

“He was… as he always is.”

That earned the faintest curve of her mouth.

“Yes,” she said. “That is severe enough.”

They walked a little farther in silence. Lucian found that the tightness behind his ribs — the one that had followed him from the terrace — eased without disappearing entirely. It was different with Lyra than with Elara. Elara’s warmth had always felt like shelter, something that stood between him and the hardness of the house. Lyra’s presence felt more like being claimed. As if he belonged in her attention the same way he belonged to the banners and the stone and the name itself.

She stopped near a recessed bench set against the wall, where the light from a high window fell in a pale rectangle across the stone. Dust hung motionless in the beam. Sitting, she brought herself closer to his height and reached out again, this time to straighten the front of his cloak more carefully. The fabric whispered faintly under her hands. She smoothed one edge, then the other, and only when both lay even did she seem satisfied.

“You should not let it hang unevenly,” she said. “Especially after seeing Father. He notices things like that.”

“I know.”

“I know you know.” Her hands paused for a moment on the fabric. “I still check.”

Lucian stood still and let her finish. There was no embarrassment in it. Only the quiet understanding that this was something she did, and something he allowed. When she was satisfied, she rested her hands in her lap and looked at him properly. The light from the window touched the edge of her hair and left the rest of her face in softer shadow.

“You carry too much of it already,” she said.

“Of what?”

“Whatever he put on you this morning.”

Lucian did not deny it. He looked down the corridor instead, where the dark stone stretched on in clean, severe lines. A faint draft moved along the floor, cold enough to find the gaps in his boots and the places where his cloak did not fully close.

“He said I will need to be strong.”

“You will.” Lyra’s answer came without hesitation. “But you are still small. He sometimes forgets that.”

The words were not rebellious. Only honest. In the quiet of the hall they sounded almost gentle.

Lucian looked back at her. For a moment he saw not only the older sister who adjusted his cloak and waited in corridors, but the shape of someone who had already decided her place in his life — between him and the harder edges of the house, whenever she could stand there.

“I’m not afraid of him,” he said.

“I know.” She reached out and briefly touched his hair, smoothing it the same way Elara sometimes did, though the gesture felt different from her hand. Cooler. More deliberate. “That isn’t always the same as not feeling the weight.”

He did not answer. He did not need to. The truth of it sat between them without requiring agreement. Outside, far beyond the stone, the wind moved against the high walls of Blackspire. Inside, the keep listened in its usual silence.

After a while, Lyra rose again. The bench was cold enough that the absence of her warmth left a faint trace in the air. She held out her hand.

“Come,” she said. “I’ll walk you the rest of the way. Then I should return before I’m missed.”

Lucian took her hand. It was warmer than the stone, cooler than Elara’s, and steady in a way that asked for nothing except that he stay within reach. Her fingers closed around his with quiet certainty, as if the matter had already been decided.

They continued together through the quieter halls of Blackspire. The keep remained cold. The banners remained still. Somewhere far above the peaks, beyond stone and cloud, Ancalion moved through the winter sky. The memory of the terrace and the land and the words of the house remained with Lucian — burden, crown, weight — but walking beside his sister, the weight did not feel quite so solitary.

Inside the keep, for a little while longer, he was only a boy being led onward by someone who had been waiting for him.
