# Chapter 13: Path

The training hall was empty.

Cold air moved through the high vents and across the bare stone floor in a slow, continuous current. Pale morning light entered in narrow bands from the upper slits and left the greater part of the chamber in clean shadow. The walls were unadorned dark stone. No banners hung here. No attendants stood at the edges. No instructor waited to correct from the side. Lucian worked alone.

He had been at the forms since before the light fully cleared the peaks. The work was not assigned for the morning. It was simply what he required of himself.

He moved through the upper sequences with exact, sustained force. Each stance was taken as if it were the only one that mattered, held until the muscles of the legs and core burned, and then held longer still. Transitions were clean and economical. When a line slipped by the smallest degree — a shoulder rising, a weight shifting a fraction too far forward — he stopped at once, reset to the opening position, and began the entire sequence again from the beginning. There was no visible frustration in the correction. Only the same measured return to the start.

Breath remained controlled even as the effort mounted. He drew air in through the nose, released it slowly, and refused to let the rhythm break. Sweat ran down his back and along his temples, cooling quickly in the unheated air. He did not wipe it away. The cold of the hall pressed against the heat of the work and made the boundary between them clear.

Time passed without marker. The only sounds were the controlled impact of bare feet on stone, the soft shift of training clothes, and the steady rhythm of his breathing. Outside the high slits the light strengthened by degrees, but inside the chamber the quality of the air and the stone remained unchanged. He finished one full set, allowed himself three slow breaths in stillness, and began the next.

When the final form of the last sequence was complete, he held the closing stance until the tremor in his legs became a distinct and separate fact. Only then did he release it. He stood in the center of the hall, feet still properly set, and brought his breathing down by deliberate degrees. The cold settled against his skin in a continuous sheet. The silence of the chamber returned in full, heavier now that the movement had stopped.

He remained there, upright and quiet, letting the residual burn in the muscles fade into a cleaner awareness of the body. The diligence of the morning did not leave with the end of the forms. It stayed in the way he held himself, in the refusal to collapse the posture even in recovery.

He was still standing in that controlled stillness when the door opened.

Vesper entered without announcement. He closed the door behind him and regarded his son for a long moment from the threshold. The presence of the lord of the house altered the air of the chamber at once — not through any outward display, but through the simple fact of his attention. Lucian turned, straightened further, and inclined his head.

“Father.”

Vesper crossed the floor at an unhurried pace. His black attire was exact as always, the silver at the cuffs the only concession to ornament. He stopped a few paces away and studied the boy in silence. His gaze moved from the controlled breathing to the residual tension still present in the frame, then to the absence of wasted motion even in recovery. He took in the sweat, the exactness of the stance, and the fact that the hall had been in use for a considerable time.

“You have been at this for some time,” Vesper said.

“Yes, Father.”

“Unassigned.”

“Yes.”

A slight inclination of Vesper’s head registered the fact without praise. He walked a slow half-circle around Lucian, boots nearly silent on the stone, then stopped again opposite him.

“The body has advanced. Precision holds under fatigue. Stillness is no longer forced.” His voice remained even, almost quiet, yet every word landed with the weight of measured judgment. “Dain’s reports and my own observation agree. You have the foundation.”

Lucian waited. He did not shift his weight. The cold air moved between them.

Vesper’s gaze remained on him.

“You also have talent for the Veil. That too is clear. The early sense was not an accident, and the discipline required to approach it without grasping has held. Both paths are open to you.”

He let the statement stand for a moment in the cold air of the hall. The thin light from the upper slits did not change. Somewhere beyond the thick stone the keep continued its ordinary life, distant and irrelevant to the space they occupied.

“The reasonable course is to choose one and master it,” Vesper continued. “Divided attention is the common road to incompleteness. A single mastery, carried to its end, is enough to make a formidable lord of this house. Most who attempt both leave themselves unfinished in each.”

Lucian’s eyes stayed on his father’s. He offered no interruption.

“There have been exceptions,” Vesper said. “A handful across the longer history of the blood and of the wider world. Those who managed to walk both paths to true mastery became figures of consequence. Legends, in the older records. There have also been far more who tried the same and failed. The records favor the failures. The cost is usually permanent.”

He regarded his son with the same measured attention he had given the forms.

“My own greater strength lay with the Veil. The choice was clearer for me. Dain possessed ability in both, as you do, and elected the combat path. You stand where he once stood — with both open before you. That is uncommon. It is also a heavier responsibility than having only one true road.”

Vesper’s voice did not change in volume or pace. The words remained factual, almost spare.

“You have the privilege of the choice. I will not make it for you. In time you will need to know what you intend to become — a master of the blade, a master of the Veil, or one of the rare who attempt both. The decision carries its own weight. That weight is already yours.”

The hall remained silent around them. The thin light from the upper slits had shifted only slightly since Vesper entered. Lucian felt the residual heat of the training still fading from his body, and beneath it the newer, cleaner weight of the question that had just been placed in front of him.

He answered without haste.

“I understand, Father.”

Vesper held his gaze a moment longer, as though confirming that the words had been received at their full value and not merely acknowledged. Then he gave a single, exact nod.

“Continue as you have been until the choice is made. The work does not pause for uncertainty.”

He turned and left the hall with the same unhurried control with which he had entered. The door closed softly behind him. The sound of the latch was final and precise.

Lucian remained where he stood.

The cold of the chamber pressed against the residual heat of the training in a continuous, equalizing sheet. The question his father had placed before him sat with the same clean weight as the stone under his feet — not urgent, not dramatic, simply present and irreversible. His father had chosen the Veil. His uncle had chosen the blade. Most who tried both failed. A handful became legends. The reasonable path was to choose one.

He drew a slow breath, settled his stance with the same exactness he had used all morning, and began the next set of forms from the beginning.

The hall stayed empty. The work continued.
