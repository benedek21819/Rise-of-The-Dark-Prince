# Chapter 11: Veil

Time had passed again in the high halls of Blackspire.

Lucian stood in one of the smaller instructional chambers cut into the upper mountain, a room of bare dark stone where the ordinary sounds of the keep fell away. The air was cold and still. A single narrow window set high in the wall admitted a thin band of pale light that crossed the floor in a clean line and then faded into the deeper gloom near the far corner. He was taller than he had been. The lines of his frame had sharpened with the months. When he settled into stillness now, it held with less effort than before; the restless adjustments of earlier years had largely quieted.

Dain entered without announcement and closed the door behind him. The sound of the latch was soft and final. He regarded Lucian for a long moment, measuring posture, breath, and the quiet already present in the boy’s body. The assessment was thorough and unhurried.

“Your forms have improved,” Dain said. The words were plain, without warmth or ceremony. “Endurance is better. You hold stillness longer when the body tires. Discipline is becoming reliable rather than forced. That is sufficient progress to take the next step.”

Lucian inclined his head once. He did not speak. He had learned that Dain preferred acknowledgment over reply.

Dain moved to the center of the chamber. His boots made almost no sound on the stone.

“Today is not about the body,” he said. “Today is about what lies beneath motion and strength. A first step only. Nothing more.”

He directed Lucian into a precise standing posture — feet set at a measured width, spine aligned, shoulders settled, hands quiet at his sides. The instructions were exact and few. Breath was to be steady and unforced. Attention was not to reach outward toward the room or the instructor. The body was to become still enough that ordinary thought loosened its grip and simply fell away.

Lucian adjusted as directed. The cold of the stone floor pressed upward through the soles of his boots. The thin light from the window did not change. He let his breath settle into a slow, even rhythm and held the posture without strain.

Dain circled him once. He corrected the angle of a shoulder with a single light touch, then stepped back to the edge of the boy’s vision.

“There is a current beneath the world,” he said. His voice remained low and practical, stripped of anything that might encourage wonder. “Most never feel it. Some learn to sense it. Fewer still learn to shape it. You will not reach for it. You will not try to take hold of it. You will only become still enough to notice whether anything answers the stillness. That is all.”

Lucian gave a slight nod.

“Begin.”

The chamber grew quieter.

Lucian held the posture as instructed. Breath moved in and out without force. The small discomforts of the body — the cold seeping through his boots, the faint accumulating strain in the legs and lower back, the awareness of Dain’s presence at the edge of the room — rose one by one and then receded as he refused to follow them with his attention. Time stretched. The thin light on the floor did not shift. The silence of the room deepened by degrees until even the distant life of the keep seemed to fall farther and farther away, as if the stone itself had decided to hold its breath.

At first there was only the ordinary quiet of stone and breath.

Then, faintly, something else.

It was not a sound. It was not a light. It came as a subtle thinning of the silence, a pressure so light it might have been imagined — as if the still air of the chamber rested against something larger and indifferent that existed just beyond the reach of ordinary perception. The sensation did not grow. It did not approach or recede. It simply was, vast and remote, like the mountain itself given another kind of presence. Lucian felt the edge of it the way one might feel the presence of a great depth beneath thin ice: not threatening, not inviting, only there. He did not reach for it. He only remained still enough to notice that it existed.

The moment lasted a measured span of breaths. Then Dain’s voice cut cleanly across it.

“Enough.”

Lucian opened his eyes. The chamber returned in full — the cold stone, the narrow band of light, the exact figure of his uncle standing a few paces away. The faint pressure was already gone, leaving only the memory of having brushed against the edge of something that did not belong to the ordinary world of corridors and training forms.

Dain studied him in silence for a moment longer.

“You did not force it,” he said. “You did not grasp. You held the stillness long enough to notice. That is the correct beginning.” A short pause. “It is also incomplete. Most who sense anything at all never go further. Many who try to go further ruin the foundation by reaching too soon. You will not reach. Not yet.”

Lucian met his eyes and waited.

“This work will return when your stillness and discipline are stronger still,” Dain continued. “Until then, the ordinary forms continue. The Veil is not a toy. It is not a reward. It is a current that will answer only when the one who approaches it has become exact enough to bear the answer.”

He gave a single nod, the instruction finished.

“You may go.”

Lucian inclined his head, turned, and left the chamber.

The corridors outside felt unchanged at first. The same thin light. The same cold in the walls. The same quiet movement of the household at its careful distance. Yet something faint from the attempt lingered — not power, not even a clear memory of sensation, only the knowledge that a deeper layer existed beneath the stone and the training he already knew. It sat lightly in him, unfinished and precise, another weight added to the quiet place where the rest of the house’s burdens already lived.

As he turned into a wider passage that led back toward the residential wing, two servants came from the opposite direction, speaking in low voices. They carried linens stacked between them and did not notice him at once.

“—more restless than usual,” one was saying. “The northern houses have been sending messages again. No one will say why. Even the stewards are being careful with their words.”

The other made a sharp, quiet sound of warning. Both fell silent the moment they saw Lucian. They stepped aside at once, eyes lowered, and waited without speaking until he had passed. Only when his footsteps had receded did the soft sound of their movement resume behind him.

Lucian did not slow. The fragment meant nothing to him. Houses sent messages. The North had always held its own quiet tensions beneath the surface of loyalty. He carried the words only as long as it took to leave the passage, then let them settle with the rest of the morning’s unfinished weight. They joined the faint memory of the current he had brushed against — present, unexplained, and not yet his concern.

He walked without hurry. At a high window overlooking the northern slopes he paused. The glass was cold under his fingertips. Beyond it the peaks stood in their long silence, white and gray beneath a sky the color of steel. Somewhere beyond ordinary sight the current Dain had spoken of moved, vast and indifferent. Lucian did not try to feel it again. He only stood there until the cold of the glass began to reach through his glove, then stepped back into the corridor and continued on.

Blackspire remained what it was. The door that had been opened was still only slightly ajar.
