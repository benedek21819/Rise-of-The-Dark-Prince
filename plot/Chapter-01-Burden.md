# Chapter 1: Burden

The corridors of Blackspire were always cold.

Lucian had learned that before he learned many other things. The stone drank the warmth from the air and gave nothing back. Even the light seemed thinner here, as though it had to earn its right to touch the walls. Tall windows of dark glass let in the pale northern sky, but the brightness never reached far. It stopped at the edges of the floor, leaving the center of the halls in a quiet, permanent dusk.

His mother’s hand was warm around his.

Lady Elara walked at an unhurried pace, her silver hair catching what little light there was. She did not seem to feel the cold the way others did. Or if she did, she refused to let it touch her voice.

“You are quiet this morning,” she said.

Lucian looked up at her. “I am always quiet.”

A soft smile touched her mouth. “Not always. Only when you are thinking too much.”

He did not deny it. He *was* thinking. About the way the servants stepped aside when they passed. About the distant sound of boots somewhere deeper in the keep. About the fact that his father had sent for him.

Elara’s thumb brushed lightly over the back of his hand.

“There is nothing to fear,” she said. “Your father only wishes to speak with you.”

Lucian nodded. He did not say that he was not afraid. Not exactly. It was something closer to caution. A tightening behind the ribs. The same feeling he got when standing too close to the edge of the high terraces, looking down at the long drop of stone and snow.

They turned a corner. The corridor widened. The ceilings rose higher, supported by dark pillars carved with severe, elegant lines. Blackspire did not decorate itself with soft things. Even its beauty was sharp.

Elara glanced down at him again.

“Your cloak is straight. Good. Stand tall when you greet him.”

“I will.”

“And listen carefully.”

“I always do.”

Her smile deepened, just a little. “Yes. You do.”

For a few steps longer, the warmth of her presence held the cold at bay. Then the air changed.

It was subtle at first. The silence grew heavier. The distant sounds of the keep seemed to fall away. Even the light from the windows felt less welcome. Lucian felt it in his shoulders before he understood it — the quiet pressure that always surrounded his father.

Lord Vesper Caldarion stood near one of the tall arched openings that led toward the outer terraces. He was dressed in black, as he almost always was, the silver at his cuffs and collar the only concession to ornament. His hair, streaked with iron-gray, was tied back cleanly. His face was calm. Controlled. Unreadable.

Elara slowed as they approached.

“My lord,” she said, her voice still soft, but more measured now.

Vesper’s gaze moved from her to Lucian. For a moment he simply looked at his son, as though weighing something silent.

“Leave us,” he said.

There was no harshness in the words. Only finality.

Elara hesitated for half a breath. Then she released Lucian’s hand, rested her fingers briefly against his shoulder, and stepped back.

“I will be nearby,” she said quietly.

Lucian watched her go. The warmth went with her.

Vesper turned toward the open archway.

“Come.”

They walked out into the cold.

The gardens of Blackspire were not soft places. They were walled and ordered, built into the mountain’s shoulder where the wind could not fully reach. Stone paths cut between hardy evergreens and pale winter blooms that clung stubbornly to life. Beyond the low walls, the world fell away into peaks and deep valleys, all of it white and gray beneath a sky the color of steel.

Lucian kept pace beside his father. His shorter legs made it difficult, but he did not complain.

For a time, Vesper said nothing. Their footsteps were the only sound.

Then:

“You are the heir of this house.”

Lucian looked up. “Yes, Father.”

“Do you know what that means?”

He thought for a moment. “That I will lead after you.”

“That is the smallest part of it.”

Vesper did not look at him as he spoke. His eyes remained on the path ahead, or on the distant mountains. His voice was even, almost quiet, but every word landed with weight.

“It means that every life within these walls will one day rest on your decisions. The guards who stand the walls. The servants who keep the fires. Your mother. Your sister. The men who will follow your banners into war. Their survival will depend on whether you are strong enough to carry what is given to you.”

Lucian’s fingers curled slightly inside his cloak.

“I will be strong,” he said.

“You will need to be.”

They walked on.

The path curved near an older section of the garden, where the stone was darker and the vines thicker. An ancient gate stood there, half-swallowed by moss. Time had worn its edges, but at its center remained a dark circular carving, with faint lines spreading outward like rays.

Lucian’s steps slowed.

“Father,” he said. “What is that?”

Vesper followed his gaze. His expression did not change.

“An old mark,” he said. “Something left from long before your time.”

“What does it mean?”

“It does not matter now.”

The answer was calm. Final. Vesper was already moving again.

Lucian looked at the gate a moment longer, then followed.

They came to the edge of the terrace, where the wall dropped away and the full sweep of the Northern Duchy lay open before them. Mountains upon mountains. Deep valleys cut by pale rivers of ice. Forests darkened by distance. Villages so far below they were little more than suggestions of shape.

Vesper rested one hand on the cold stone of the wall.

“Look,” he said.

Lucian looked.

“Everything you see,” Vesper continued, “will one day be yours. The peaks. The valleys. The people who live in their shadow. The strength of this house. The fear it commands. All of it.”

The wind moved across the terrace, sharp and clean.

“But it is not a gift,” Vesper said. “It is a burden. A lord who cannot protect what is his does not deserve to hold it. Do you understand?”

Lucian stared out at the vast, cold land. It seemed endless. Too large for his hands. Too heavy for his shoulders. And yet something in his chest answered the sight of it — a quiet, steady pressure that was not entirely fear.

“Yes, Father,” he said.

Vesper glanced down at him then. For a brief moment, something unreadable passed through his eyes. Not warmth. Not pride, exactly. Something closer to recognition.

“Good,” he said.

They stood in silence after that, father and son, looking out over the territory that would one day demand everything of the boy beside him.
